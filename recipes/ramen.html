Tonkotsu
Ramen

Prep Time 30 min
Cook Time 12–14 hours
Serves 4 bowls
Difficulty Advanced

Description

Twelve Hours of Patience, One Perfect Bowl

Tonkotsu is not a quick recipe — it is a commitment. Pork trotters and back bones, blasted at a furious boil for the better part of a day, release their collagen into the liquid until it turns silky, creamy, and opaque. The result is a broth that coats every strand of noodle and every spoonful feels like the whole of winter being lifted from your shoulders.

Topped with chashu pork belly, a ramen egg marinated overnight in soy and mirin, sliced scallions, sheets of nori, and a drizzle of sesame oil — this bowl is a project. Start it the morning before you plan to eat. You will not regret it.

Ingredients

What You'll Need

  • 1 kg pork trotters, cut
  • 500 g pork neck bones
  • 500 g pork belly (chashu)
  • 4 portions fresh ramen noodles
  • 4 large eggs
  • 80 ml soy sauce
  • 60 ml mirin
  • 2 tbsp sake
  • 1 tsp sugar
  • 1 whole head garlic, halved
  • 5 cm fresh ginger, sliced
  • 4 spring onions (whites for broth, greens for topping)
  • 1 tbsp sesame oil
  • 4 sheets toasted nori
  • 1 tbsp white sesame seeds
  • Toasted sesame paste (tare), 2 tsp per bowl
  • Salt, to taste

Method

Step by Step

  1. Blanch the trotters and neck bones: place in a pot, cover with cold water, bring to a hard boil, and cook for 10 minutes. Drain and rinse bones thoroughly under cold running water. This removes impurities and gives you a cleaner broth.

  2. Return the cleaned bones to your largest pot and cover with 4 litres of fresh water. Bring to a vigorous boil, then add the garlic head, ginger, and spring onion whites. Reduce to a steady, energetic simmer (not rolling, but active). Cook for 10–12 hours, topping up with water as needed to keep bones submerged. The broth will turn creamy and opaque — this is the goal.

  3. While the broth cooks, make the chashu: roll the pork belly tightly, secure with butcher's twine. Sear in a hot oiled pan until golden on all sides. Transfer to a saucepan, add 60 ml soy sauce, 60 ml mirin, 2 tbsp sake, 1 tsp sugar, and enough water to half-submerge the pork. Simmer covered on very low heat for 90 minutes. Let cool in liquid. Refrigerate overnight if possible.

  4. Make the ramen eggs: bring a pot of water to a boil. Gently lower cold eggs in and cook for exactly 6 minutes 30 seconds. Transfer to an ice bath immediately. Peel and marinate in a mixture of 3 tbsp soy sauce, 2 tbsp mirin, and 100 ml water for at least 4 hours (overnight is ideal).

  5. Strain the finished broth through a fine sieve, pressing the bones to extract every drop. Season with salt. The broth should be rich, creamy, and intensely savoury.

  6. To assemble: warm 4 bowls. Add ½ tsp sesame paste (tare) to each. Ladle about 350 ml of hot broth into each bowl and stir to dissolve the tare. Cook noodles per package instructions, drain well, and place in each bowl.

  7. Top each bowl with 2–3 slices of chashu (seared briefly in a hot pan to caramelise), one halved ramen egg, sliced spring onion greens, a sheet of nori standing upright, sesame seeds, and a drizzle of sesame oil. Serve immediately while steaming hot.
